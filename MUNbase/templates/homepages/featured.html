Kremlin vs. Navalny: drone footage, nerve agents, and a bid to expose the darkest secrets of Russian Politics

By Ahaan Jindal

What’s happening in Russia right now?

Opposition leader Alexei Navalny is probably sitting in a nondescript, derelict jail cell, facing parole violation and embezzlement charges, grappling with back pain, sleeplessness, and the maddening constancy of being monitored every hour by prison guards

At the same time, the world outside the facility seems to be enmeshed within the throes of a political crisis, with protests as strong as 400,000 people and police detainment raging throughout the streets of Moscow and beyond. With a resounding 52% of Russians feeling like Navalny’s incarceration was unjust and a politically motivated ploy, coupled with rising disillusionment over a Russian economic collapse precipitated by plummeting oil prices, an intricate web of corruption, and lockdown restrictions, it is safe to say that Putin’s political system is in a rather precarious situation.

Some context - the House that Putin built and Navalny’s growing traction

Putin started off as a KGB operative, eventually rising through the ranks and retiring from the Soviet Secret police in 1991, to make a foray into Russian politics. He worked closely under incumbent Leningrad (now St Petersburg) mayor Anatoly Sobchak as Deputy Mayor, and, by 1996, became the head of the FSB, the organization that replaced the post-Soviet KGB. During his tenure, he managed to gain the support of the “oligarchs” - wealthy owners of important Russian business sectors such as energy and telecommunications - by supporting the existence of monopolies and accusing their competitors of fraud and money laundering. Putin also gained valuable ties to Russian Security Officers and Crime Organizations, paving the way for him to become a frontrunner for higher-ranking positions. Putin’s time came in 1999, when incumbent president Boris Yeltsin, appointed Putin as acting president, a move many deemed was to protect himself from rising public outcry after Russian forces failed to counter secular separatists during the Chechnya War. Putin subsequently won the elections in March 2000, and there was no looking back for the former KGB spy.

Putin then began to model his policy endeavors and presidential rule based on one sole, focused aim - to control over every facet of his country - and set out to achieve this consolidation of power by gaining control of all media avenues, sending police officers to independent news agencies, publishers, and television agencies. He used this influence over sources of media to restrict free speech, and sway elections through propaganda. News Channels such as RT and TV Tsentr became either state-funded or state-run, and effectively turned into propaganda machines that painted Putin as an all-powerful leader, playing a significant role in Putin’s skyrocketing approval ratings, as well as electoral success, with ~63% of the vote in the 2012 elections. Putin also leveraged the mass-media to quell any semblance of political dissent as well as a range of human rights and social liberties, a striking example of which is his scorching campaign against a prominent Russian gay rights and feminist group, sentencing 3 members to 2 years in prison. More importantly, however, were Putin’s links with the Russian oligarchy he had developed during his tenure as Deputy Mayor of St Petersburg. While he brutally got rid of certain oligarchs who associated with any opposition parties, such as Mikhail Khodorkhovsky and Nikolay Glushkov, by prosecuting them for bogus embezzlement charges, he used the others to seize control of the judicial system, and fund what - in the words of historian and journalist Masha Gesser - was the “core of the [Putin’s] regime”: corruption. A 2011 report by Global Financial Integrity (GFI) revealed that a mammoth $427 billion was lost in what GFI termed “illicit cash outflows siphoned off to offshore accounts.” According to Sergei Ivanov, the Kremlin Chief of Staff, the most “corrupted spheres” in Russia’s government are law enforcement companies, healthcare and pharmaceutical monopolies, as well as government licenses and contracts for infrastructure. This intricate web of corruption - wherein monopolies and wealthy business owners alike pay hefty sums to Putin and those at the top at Kremlin in exchange for any legal/political favours, is essentially the binding force of his entire administration, and what keeps Putin in power.

This gradual seizure of state control - through facets such as media control, electoral interference and doctoring, control of judicial courts, support of the oligarchs, and, most importantly, corruption - has been extremely well-hidden by Putin; there is, however, a hitch in his plan for complete Russian dominance and keeping this system alive until 2036 (when Putin can legally no longer be President) - a young lawyer named Alexei Navalny.

Navalny has been an active member of the Russian opposition movement since 2011, before which he conducted in-depth investigations into the inherent corruption and bribery that held Putin’s regime together, concealed by the state media, and seemingly oblivious to the judicial courts. The first investigation he conducted was in 2010, when he discovered - after buying stocks in the state-owned and funded transportation company Transneft and going over internal financial documents - that government officials had siphoned off approximately $4 billion into offshore accounts possessed by Transneft officials. He then began conducting more such investigations into state-owned monopolies in various sectors, noticing this web of corruption in oil companies, banks, and companies owned by the oligarchs who supported Putin. This threatened to reveal Putin’s entire political system and it’s darkest intricacies, and soon began piquing the interest of the Russian people, who slowly began joining large-scale protests, starting in 2011, which were often organized and spearheaded by Navalny. What separated Navalny from other opposition leaders was his willfulness - while Kremlin managed to either silence, exile or execute them, Navalny stayed put and continued to circumvent Putin’s ploys to achieve his goal of exposing the system that kept Putin in power for 20+ years.

By 2013, Navalny managed to gain enough traction to run for Mayor of Moscow. While his campaign was enshrouded by controversy (seeing as he had, in the past, used racial slurs against ethnic minorities, often participated in nationalist movements, and even supported the annexation of Crimea - all extremely right wing actions), he garnered enough support to effectively run his campaign, through online crowdsourcing platforms, rallies, and volunteers, since Putin’s stronghold over the media ensured that Navalny got no air time on television. His campaign was soon cut short owing to his arrest following false allegations of embezzlement; Navalny was sentenced to 5 years in jail, but was eventually let go on bail. Navalny then proceeded to run for President against Putin, but was yet again stopped by Putin’s regime, who cited his previous “criminal” history as evidence for his disqualification, which exposed the inherent electoral doctoring and corruption that marred Russia’s political system.

Navalny continued to propagate his agenda through a rather unconventional avenue - social media platforms, such as YouTube, Instagram, and TikTok - by creating short videos that, whilst hilariously mocking oligarchs or Putin himself, provided convincing evidence of corruption and wrongdoing in the Russian government. He even continued to expose the intricacies of the Russian Government following his poisoning in October 2020, with his team releasing a documentary on YouTube revealing drone footage of a large estate on the shores of the Black Sea, which was reportedly owned by Putin. Calling the mansion “Putin’s Palace”, Navalny and his team analyzed every aspect of the house, and confirmed the presence of luxuries such as an Amphitheatre, casinos, a hockey rink, and a tea house, among others - deemed a “waste of Russian’s taxpayer money.”

Navalny’s poisoning and its aftermath

In August 2020, during a routine 4-hour flight from Tomsk to Moscow, Navalny became violently ill, and prompted an emergency landing to Omsk, where Navalny was transferred to City Clinical Emergency Hospital Number 1, where Navalny was put in a medically-induced coma, and on a ventilator. Doctors at the hospital tested him for the presence of toxins, but denied the presence of any poison. Navalny’s wife, Yulia Navalnaya, later tried entering the hospital with their family doctor, Anastasia Vasilyeva, but was coerced to show their marriage certificate as authentication. Later, a German charity foundation called the Cinema for Peace Foundation sent a chartered plane to airlift Navalny to Berlin for further treatment. Doctors eventually concluded that Navalny had been poisoned with cholinesterase inhibitors, and treated Navalny with medicines to counter nerve agents, enabling Navalny to recuperate quickly.

Investigations later conducted by the German government, in conjunction with the Russian Anti-Corruption Foundation, or FBK, later revealed that Navalny had been poisoned with nerve agent Novichok, which the USSR had, during the Cold War, developed during the 1970s to incapacitate political opponents. Navalny also managed to trick a Russian government agent assigned to carry out his poisoning to reveal its details, and concluded that Novichok had actually been added to his underwear before his flight to Moscow.

He eventually returned to Moscow in January 2021, after making a complete recovery, and was promptly arrested by Russian officials on account of reported “parole violations” following his 2014 embezzlement charge.

What’s next for the country and its opposition movement

Navalny’s incarceration has stoked the flames of the public, with members of the Middle-class and the youth holding protests throughout the country. Putin’s approval rating - which once comfortably stayed above 80% - continues to plummet significantly, owing to economic issues, as well as Navalny’s efforts to reveal the components of Kremlin’s rule that have kept Putin in power, a “house of cards” destined to fall. Navalny’s health in the prison facility unfortunately continues to deteriorate, with his doctor revealing to the public that his death is imminent - but the impact he has had so far on Russian politics is indelible, and may be the ideal foundation for breaking down the Russian “House of Cards.” Navalny 1, Kremlin 0.

Bengal succumbs to the Saffron Wave?

By Arnav Joshi

Easily among the most gruesome and neck to neck elections over the last two decades, the Bengal elections finally began polling last week. On 27th march, 30 constituencies in Bengal went to the booth, with 191 candidates competing for these seats. This marked the beginning of an election, which has been prefaced with opposition being tear gassed, chief ministers getting physically attacked (allegedly at least) the families of political workers getting attacked, the list goes on. The discussion of this article, however, is going to be a discussion of the claims made by the two political stalwarts battling it out, Mamata Banerjee and Amit Shah. Union Home Minister Amit Shah claims that the BJP will win 26 out of the 30 seats in phase one of the elections, whereas incumbent chief minister Banerjee believes that higher voter turnout is a clear sign of a pro incumbency sentiment. Let’s look at which of these two sides really will end up reigning supreme. Will Didi retain her prized possession, or will the saffron engulf yet another state.

First things first, yes, this is a purely two horse race. The left alliance does in fact have some sway over the final results in seats and could be the deciding X factor, however thinking that they can win would be wishful thinking at best and naivety at worst from their end. The best possible outcome for the left would be for neither the BJP nor the TMC to get an absolute majority, hence giving the left a possible bargaining chip for the formation of a Mahagathbandhan of sorts.

First off, what the BJP has going for it, is its traditional no stone left unturned mindset. The BJPs dedication towards political advancements has always allowed them to treat every election in a do or die manner. When the BJP sees any chance of winning an election, the prime minister, the home minister, the president of the BJP, and other BJP stalwarts are seen campaigning day in and day out. No prize is too small for the BJPs work, and no government is too stable for the BJP to attempt destabilisation. Both of these were exemplified by the parties orchestrated breakdown of the Puducherry government. Both Amit Shah and Narendra Modi himself have been campaigning themselves in Bengal day in and day out in the month leading up to the elections. This is a degree of 'star power' that no other party can achieve. The power of a Modi led campaign has been seen in 2014 and 2019 in the general elections, 2017 in UP, and most recently, in 2020 in Bihar where Narendra Modi was the face of the campaign while Nitish Kumar sat back. Thus, the BJP have their ever so active attitude going for them.

The BJP has another thing going for them, their ability to construe themselves as a pan India party. Through this, they have managed to keep on the low the "outsiders" narrative. What the BJP have going for them is the fact that despite technically running a campaign led by two individuals largely based in Delhi and Gujarat, they have managed to dismiss the outsider’s narrative as rather cliche, arguing that the Hindu nationalist party is so pervasive, that every part of the Hindustan is where they very much belong. This, coupled with the Hindutva vote bank they have, consisting of majorly middle class and upper middle-class Hindus, leaves little for Mamata Banerjee to harp onto. She could’ve used her Bengal Ki Beti image, but in the year leading up to the elections the Bhartiya Janata party had made it a point to co-opt that identity as well. Subtle references to Bengali culture such as appreciation for Netaji Subhash Chandra Bose, Rabindranath Tagore etc, were all it took to get the two giants on an almost equal footing when it comes to the cultural power they wield.

The BJP, however, have one clear area where they are lacking, which is something Mamata Banerjee will be hoping plays a big role. The 'national security' and 'Pakistani' narrative. Taking the four major elections as an example, General elections of 2014 and 19, Uttar Pradesh elections of 2017, and Bihar 2020, we can see how important these narratives are. During the 2014 elections, BJP used the argument that the congress had been soft on Pakistan and soft on terror, something they wanted to change as laid out in their manifesto. Similarly, during the 2017 Uttar Pradesh elections, the BJP used their Surgical strikes in Pakistan as the narrative to support their hard stance against any threat to Indian Sovereignty. The Balakot air strike served the same purpose during the 2019 general elections, and in 2020 Bihar elections the BJP put forth the argument that an RJD victory would be the equivalent of a Diwali gift which will be celebrated in Pakistan. One can’t help but notice a trend.... This time over, however, they have failed to use one of their biggest selling points. This could work against them.

Finally, what Banerjee will be counting the most on is the historical context and contemporary paradigm in which this elect. The BJP usually has one or both of two things going for themselves. Firstly, the BJP usually dominates in states that share the Hindi heartlands philosophies and belief systems. In states where this does not work, they hope to have an opposition that has neither an ideology more influential than Hindutva, nor a socio-religious vote bank that the opposition can rely on. In states where this environment doesn’t exist, BJP tends to underperform, as seen in South India. Firstly, Bengal has its own ideals, believes and political ecosystem, one based on Bengali heritage and pride, one completely different from that of the Hindi heartland. Secondly, Mamata Banerjee being a ten-year incumbent right-wing leader would be more likely to draw the moderate Hindu vote bank, and will be hoping to cutting into the OBC pie as made clear by the TMC manifesto. This might create a road block for the BJP.

When it boils down to it however, this will be an extremely close election. Current media predictions suggest that it will be a narrow BJP victory, and while that is a safe pick, I believe the initial Left road block I mentioned might play a pivotal role. If they manage to take away 35 odd seats, there may not end up being a party with the majority. We might really end up having a loose Gathbandhan with the left holding all the cards.



The Syrian Civil War – 10 Years On...

By Jash Bhatia

A peaceful uprising against the then President of Syria in 2011 escalated into a massive civil war. 10 years on, the battle has not ceased.

Under President Bashar al-Assad, the Syrian citizens had been subjected to high unemployment rates, corruption, and political unrest. Finally, in 2011, the nation reached its tipping point and pro-democracy revolts began stirring up all over the country. Meanwhile, the Syrian government used deadly force to crush the rebellions as the people protested demanding the resignation of the president. The supporters of the opposition parties began taking up arms to protect themselves as the crackdown intensified.

Rebel groups sprang up all around the country as violence escalated – and the foreign powers of the world came into play. Mr Assad promised this “foreign-backed terrorism” would be crushed, as the civil war became much more than a battle between Syrians for or against the President. Extremist organizations such as the IS and al-Qaeda became involved, which prompted concern among the international community.

With hundreds of thousands of deaths, over 2 million civilians have sufferedinjuries and disabilities as a result of the conflict. Over 55% of Syrians have been uprooted from their homes, and even medical facilities have been attacked. UN war crimes investigators have accused all parties of perpetrating "the most heinous violations". "Syrians," their latest report says, "have suffered vast aerial bombardments of densely populated areas; they have endured chemical weapons attacks and modern day sieges in which perpetrators deliberately starved the population along medieval scripts and indefensible and shameful restrictions on humanitarian aid."

More recently, the government has regained control of Syria's biggest cities, but large parts of the country are still held by rebels, jihadists and the Kurdish-led SDF. The last remaining opposition stronghold is in the province of Idlib and parts of northern Hama and western Aleppo provinces.

The region is dominated by a jihadist alliance called Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS), but is also home to mainstream rebel factions. An estimated 2.7 million displaced people, including a million children, are living there, many of them in dire conditions in camps.

In March 2020, Russia and Turkey brokered a ceasefire to halt a push by the government to retake Idlib. There has been a relative calm since then, but it could break down at any moment. In the country's north-east, Turkish forces and allied Syrian rebels launched an offensive against the SDF in October 2019 to create a "safe zone" clear of Kurdish YPG militia along the Syrian side of the border, and have occupied it since.

The UN Security Council has called for the implementation of the 2012 Geneva Communiqué, which implies formation of a transitional governing body "formed on the basis of mutual consent". Nine rounds of UN-mediated peace talks - known as the Geneva II process - failed to make progress, with President Assad apparently unwilling to negotiate with political opposition groups that insist he must step down as part of any settlement.

In 2021, the situation has far from settled down even remotely. Syria lies in ruins, and Assad, the perpetrator of hundreds of war crimes, remains adamant that he will not back down until his lust for power is satisfied. His regime is using all the funding it receives not for welfare purposes, but as a weapon for war, something that the United Nations has shamefully failed to prevent.

The deplorable state of affairs and widespread suffering in Syria raises just one question: has Assad won anything? The answer, it is feared, is a resounding no. The regime has gained nothing. There are no victors in war, only victims and perpetrators.





The Assam Elections - coalitions, calumny and a contentious scrimmage to claw back power

By Ahaan Jindal

Who’s vying for control - the major parties

Pictures of Rahul Gandhi unveiling the Indian National Congress’ (INC) manifesto in Assam were made perspicuously evident on major national news channels: an indication of the Mahajoth’s - or INC coalition’s - commitment to regaining Assam, where the BJP had gained power in 2016, avenging 2011’s landslide Congress victory. The INC continues to garner traction in Assam, with a cascade of calumnious comments taking a dig at BJP’s allegedly nationalistic stance in the state coming from Rahul Gandhi: “they are attacking the culture, brotherhood and diversity in Assam.” At the same time, however, the incumbent party - the BJP’s coalition along with the Asom Gana Parishad (AGP) and the United People’s Party Liberal (UPPL) - are also wilful in their goal to keep Chief Minister Sarbananda Sonowal in power, and regain the 126 MLA quota for the state’s 15th Legislative Assembly. Modi too took particular umbrage to the Congress’ exploits into the region, deeming the centre-left party’s policies “a display of their lack of leadership, policies and ideology.

With polling commencing on 1st April, and fervent campaigning by three sides of the political spectrum in a post-Covid environment marred by instability - the BJP, Congress, and the United Regional Front - a “fringe” party vying to make inroads in the Northeast - the ensuing article aims to chronicle the policies and ideologies of each major front, at the eve of the Assam elections.

What voters want

A cursory glance at Assam’s economic parameters post-Covid highlights unemployment as a key issue plaguing the economy, with unemployment surging to 11.1% in April 2020. This was accompanied by a supply-chain disruption in the region, yielding a host of particularly pernicious implications for the retail and hospitality sector, as per the state’s finance minister, Himanta Biswa Sharma. Therefore, in the short-term, prospective voters would look quite favourably upon ensuring that job security is prioritized by the next cabinet, and solutions to supply and demand “shocks'' are wheedled out before vicious cycles could emerge and ravage Assam’s now-precarious economy. The tea garden workers will also prove significant to the final results, constituting a 17% majority in Assam’s population, and have actively contested lowered wages; while the Union Budget allocated a 1000 crore tranche of funds to the workers, representatives have yet complained about insufficient wages, and will seek promises of wage increases from either party. A more perennial predicament is the rampant lack of accessibility and interconnectivity in the region, which voters hope can be ring-fenced by significant infrastructure advancements. From a socio-cultural standpoint, the prevailing sentiment in the region is that the CAA needs to be repealed, made quite conspicuous by statewide December 2019 protests in the region. Assam in particular is a state wherein the implications of the CAA are the most pertinent and considerable, seeing as it was the only Indian state to update the National Register of Citizens (NRC), rendering 19 lakh inhabitants in the region “foreigners” - what was construed to be a blow for Assam’s ethnic diversity.

The BJP’s bid to stay in control - their policies and ideology

Firstly, the BJP’s policies in the region are mainly centred around a proliferation of infrastructure in the region, coupled with a constancy of “identity politics.” The former is evidenced by the fact that from 2016 till date, Modi has been increasing his focus on developing a slew of infrastructural establishments, ranging from longer, more interconnected roads, to schools and medical colleges, such as a 500-bed Nalbari Medical College and Hospital, for which the BJP has allocated a substantial 40 crore rupees. This largely infrastructural-focused stance stems from a severe lack of interconnectivity and what seemed like a significant topographical disadvantage for Assam in the years prior, costing the region employment opportunities as well as sociocultural integration and development. An advantage for BJP hence stems from increasing their electoral base through infrastructural consolidation - with a mammoth 37% of all highway ground built in the Seven Sisters between 2014 and 2019, as well as grandiose educational and medical edifices such as the National Law School and Lakhimpur Hospital, voters who earlier suffered from unemployment and impoverishment are gravitating towards the appeal of the BJP. The local perspectives echoed by the people of Assam largely favour this developmental phase brought to the table by the BJP; a striking example of this is shopkeeper Arun Barman’s take on why he will vote for the BJP: “There is so much work that has happened under them, especially road construction. This is something Assam needed badly. Look at our upcoming medical college, for instance, see how big and crucial it is.”

A far more ideologically-fuelled policy development in the region, however, is the public condemnation of Muslim Bangladeshis, who have seemingly “infiltrated” the region - another blatantly clear instance of the BJP’s “Hindutva” manifesto. The conservative nationalist party promises to “correct and reconcile” the currently updated Assamese NRC, and have declared that they will “exclude” all “illegal migrants” and “protect genuine Indian citizens.” This clear leaning towards the Hindutva playbook is not surprising at all, given the party’s extreme stance against Muslim All India United Democratic Front (AIUDC) leader Badruddin Ajmal, with derogatory comments such as “Ajmal...is not creating a network conducive to Assamese culture. Not as an individual, but as a group of certain people, they are our enemy,” coming from BJP Minister Himanta Biswa Sarma. This apparent vendetta is clearly symbolic of BJP’s religious-secularism in the region, indicating that the BJP wishes to gain the traction of Hindu conservatives throughout the region, by appealing to the “pathos” of identity and oneness in the region. This has ostensibly propelled a significant proportion of religion-based policies: the BJP, aside from reforming the NRC, also wishes to protect Neo-Vaishnavite monasteries in the region, and solely provide land to “Indian citizens” - a bid to expand their vote banks to a vast majority of Hindus.

From a social viewpoint, the BJP has, through the “ten commitment manifesto”, or “10 Sankalp for Atmanirbhar Assam”, promised to provide free education to every child, and have focused on developing schemes to reduce the material, human and geological impact of floods in the region, in light of the catastrophic 2020 Assam Floods, which claimed 149 lives and adversely impacted 30 out of 33 Assamese districts. Furthermore, the commitments also give assurance that families who fall under the “Orunodoi” scheme, which provides Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) to around 17 lakh families in the state, will see a 261% increase in DBT, plus land rights, if the BJP comes into power. These social endeavours greatly mirror the ideological aim of “Atma Nirbhar”, or self-sufficiency for the region, and have played a significant role in polling numbers already, evidenced by the 42.1% BJP support in the exit polls.

In conclusion, the BJP has, through its infrastructural and developmental, religious, and social policies - greatly oriented to fit their nationalistic, Hindutva ideology, as well as their Atma Nirbhar campaign - aimed to expand their voter base and re-claim the treasured 126 MLA quota the largest of the “seven sisters” of the Northeast has to offer.

Congress’ bid to regain a former 15-year stronghold: politics, ideology, and destructive slander

The Congress coalition’s (Mahajoth) economic policies in the region seem to greatly appeal to two important groups of people who hold significant bearing on electoral results: the unemployed, as well as the Tea Garden workers of Assam, who hold the key to 40 seats in the region, and a substantial 5000 crores of the state income. With regard to bolstering employment in the region, the Mahajoth, under its released 5-pronged manifesto, have promised 5 lakh jobs if they are voted into power, presenting what seems to be a strong determinant in tilting the balance more towards the Centre-left party in the exit polls, by circumventing the constant near-10% unemployment in the region post-Covid. Moreover, in order to expand their electoral reach towards the tea garden workers, Congress has guaranteed a significant 120% rise in workers’ wages, to Rs. 365 per person, and have further aimed to garner their traction by echoing the issues brought forth by numerous workers’ representatives after the BJP reneged on its promise of increasing the tea workers’ wages to Rs. 367. Finally, the Congress also assured that they will provide a Rs. 2000 monthly income support to homemakers. Therefore, the Congress economic proclamation in their 5-point plan for Assam seems to appeal more to employment opportunities, and work and interaction at the grassroots level to muster traction from the tea garden workers to swing the electoral balances in their favour.

From a religious viewpoint, the Mahajoth’s leaders have repeatedly condemned the Hindutva-leaning ideological stance of the BJP in Assam, deeming it a policy that fails to rekindle the spirit of brotherhood and diversity in the region. Therefore, it comes as no surprise that the INC and its coalition parties have made their desire to repeal the CAA abundantly evident in rallies throughout February and March 2021. The ringleaders of the coalition have also, in speeches given throughout the campaigning period, dealt blows and highlighted what they see as great flaws in the BJP’s cultural nationalism - perhaps viewing it as a chance to increase their vote-banks to include proportions of the states 3.12 crore Muslim population, who seek asylum in the region, bound by the shackles of the CAA and NRC. That being said, the Mahajoth has not pinned a lot of its electoral focus on “identity politics,” instead preferring to offer the overarching aim of repealing the CAA in Assam - making clear that their agenda in the region transcends religious boundaries, and focuses more on socioeconomic amelioration.

With regard to the social manifesto, the Congress seems to target issues on a grassroots level, and have actively campaigned in various districts, to considerably improve the lives and conditions for workers, mirroring their left-leaning ideology. Priyanka Gandhi Vadra, for example, visited the Sadhguru tea gardens in Assam earlier this month, and plucked tea leaves in solidarity with the disillusioned tea workers, to further reinforce the INC’s stance: “While they [BJP] have the money and the administration, we are campaigning better at the grassroots level.” With this stance firmly in mind, the Mahajoth has also actively rallied against surging fuel prices, holding statewide protests, some of which were led by MP Gaurav Gogoi, and have repeatedly alleged that the BJP has blatantly ignored the vicissitudes in fuel prices - yet another important social talking-point of the upcoming elections. In addition, the Mahajoth coalition also aims to provide free electricity upto 200 units in certain select districts in Assam, as mentioned by Rahul Gandhi in a March 2021 rally in Guwahati, in order to further their mass appeal.

In conclusion, while the BJP’s main economic focus in Assam is infrastructure and increased interconnectivity, the Congress-led coalition has aimed to address the Covid-19-induced unemployment plaguing the entire state, and also various socioeconomic concerns; they also seem to not be excessively involved in “identity politics” and religious undertones, and as of now are fundamentally opposed to the CAA.

Conclusion - what the polls indicate and a summary of the contesting parties

The elections of Assam are one that is significant in the landscape of Indian politics, in that it will establish lasting patterns of leadership in the largest, most influential state of the Northeast: it may turn into a swing state for the May 2024 elections, or a BJP stronghold. The exit polls so far appear extremely closely contested, with a mere 2% difference between the BJP and Congress coalitions. A third party - the United Regional Front - led by jailed Marxist leader Akhil Gogoi - yet lags significantly behind in the exit polls, presumably because of its prioritization of religious issues, and an anti-CAA position that greatly coincides with Congress. To conclude, both the the BJP - which have offered a range of infrastructural developments, social improvements, and a nationalistic stance - and the Congress - which promises to reverse unemployment and offers a range of social schemes - have campaigned actively over the course of the past few months, supported by their coalitions, slandering the opposition with calumny, and, of course, have put on a contentious scrimmage to claw back power.
